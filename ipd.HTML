ECONOMY WARD

Economy Ward The most affordable hospital ward with basic healthcare facilities. Accommodates multiple patients in a shared space with minimal privacy. Basic medical equipment is provided, but facilities may be limited. Suitable for patients with non-critical conditions requiring general medical care. Often has common washrooms and shared nursing staff. Lower cost makes it accessible to a larger population.

STANDARD WARD

Standard Ward Offers better comfort and amenities compared to the economy ward. Usually has fewer beds per room, providing more privacy for patients. Equipped with better medical facilities and individual storage spaces. Suitable for patients requiring moderate medical attention but not intensive care. May have attached washrooms, personal lockers, and improved hygiene standards. Nursing staff availability is higher than in economy wards, ensuring better care. Costs more than an economy ward but remains affordable for many patients. Ideal for patients who need hospitalization but do not require specialized equipment

ICU

ICU (Intensive Care Unit) Designed for critically ill or severely injured patients requiring constant monitoring. Equipped with advanced medical technology like ventilators, cardiac monitors, and infusion pumps. Provides a high nurse-to-patient ratio for continuous care and immediate intervention. Patients in ICU often have life-threatening conditions such as organ failure, severe infections, or post-surgical complications. Restricted visitor access to maintain a sterile and controlled environment. Doctors and specialists are available 24/7 for emergency medical attention. Expensive due to specialized care, equipment, and highly trained medical staff. Ensures the highest level of medical supervision for critically ill patients

NICU

NICU (Neonatal Intensive Care Unit) Specialized unit for premature, low birth weight, or critically ill newborns. Equipped with incubators, ventilators, and advanced monitoring systems to support fragile infants. Provides round-the-clock care by neonatologists, pediatricians, and specially trained nurses. Helps manage respiratory distress, infections, jaundice, and other neonatal complications. Controlled environment with strict hygiene to prevent infections. Parental access is limited but encouraged for bonding and breastfeeding support. High-cost unit due to specialized care and advanced medical equipment. Essential for improving survival and health outcomes in high-risk newborns.
